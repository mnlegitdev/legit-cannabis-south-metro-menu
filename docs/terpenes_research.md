# The Complete Cannabis Terpene Reference Guide

**TL;DR**
- This guide profiles 22 cannabis terpenes — name, aroma, effects, indica/sativa/hybrid associations with named strain examples, food/plant sources, and boiling points — followed by a synergy ("entourage effect") section grounded in Russo (2011, *Br J Pharmacol* 163:1344–1364), Russo (2019, *Front Plant Sci* 9:1969), and Kamal et al. (2018, *Front Neurosci* 12:730).
- The single most useful budtender takeaway: **terpene profile predicts experience better than the indica/sativa label** (Smith et al. 2022, *PLOS ONE* 17:e0267498, n≈90,000 samples: "commercial labels do not consistently align with the observed chemical diversity"), and **boiling point dictates the vape temperature** that preserves a given terpene — myrcene at ~167 °C/334 °F vs. β-caryophyllene at ~266 °C/480 °F radically change what reaches the consumer's lungs.
- Outside β-caryophyllene's documented CB2 agonism (Gertsch et al. 2008, *PNAS*), most terpene "effects" are preclinical, animal-model, or correlational; treat the entourage combinations below as evidence-informed guidance, not clinical claims.

## Key Findings

1. **β-Caryophyllene is uniquely a "dietary cannabinoid"** — the only common cannabis terpene that binds CB2 receptors directly (Gertsch et al. 2008, *PNAS*; Ki ≈ 155 nM), supplying non-psychoactive anti-inflammatory activity. FDA GRAS as a food flavor.
2. **Myrcene is dominant in roughly 40% of commercial cannabis flower** per Leafly's lab-database analysis (Kaplan & Jikomes): "If you picked a random flower product off of a shelf in a legal state, you could expect it to be myrcene-dominant about 40% of the time." Concentrations above ~0.5% correlate anecdotally with "couch-lock" sedation (Russo 2011).
3. **Terpinolene-dominant cannabis is paradoxically uplifting** despite isolated terpinolene being mildly sedative in mice (Ito & Ito 2013). Terpinolene is dominant in only ~10% of strains (Leafly strain-database aggregate; "around only one in ten strains are known to be terpinolene-dominant").
4. **trans-Nerolidol — not myrcene — was the strongest correlate of anxiety relief** among Kush-family chemovars in Kamal et al. (2018), an underappreciated finding for medical-cannabis pairing.
5. **Indica/sativa is not a reliable predictor of terpene profile.** Smith, Vergara, Keegan & Jikomes (2022, *PLOS ONE* 17:e0267498) — covering ~90,000 samples across six US states — concluded "commercial labels do not consistently align with the observed chemical diversity." Lab COAs and dominant terpene listings outperform shelf-label genetics for predicting effects.

## Details

### How to Use This Guide

For each terpene below: **Aroma/Flavor → Effects → Strain Associations (indica/sativa/hybrid) → Food & Plant Sources → Boiling Point**. Boiling points are reported from peer-reviewed compendia and laboratory data (Alchimia, Finest Labs, *Terpenes and Testing Magazine*, Tamarack Cannabis lab data) and represent atmospheric-pressure values; effective vaporization usually begins slightly below these temperatures.

### 1. Myrcene (β-myrcene)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Earthy, musky, clove-like, ripe tropical fruit (mango), with hoppy/herbal undertones. The dominant "dank" baseline note in cannabis.
- **Effects:** Sedative, muscle-relaxant, analgesic, anti-inflammatory; widely credited with cannabis's "couch-lock" body high. Russo (2011) cites do Vale et al. (2002) showing myrcene potentiates barbiturate sleep time and acts as a muscle relaxant in mice.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Dominant in ~40% of commercial flower (Leafly database) across all categories, but heaviest in indica-leaning chemovars.
  - **Indica:** Granddaddy Purple, Bubba Kush, Grape Ape, Northern Lights, Purple Punch.
  - **Hybrid:** Blue Dream, OG Kush, Wedding Cake, Mango Kush.
  - **Sativa:** Tangie (lower), some Haze crosses.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Hops, mango, lemongrass, thyme, bay leaves, basil, verbena.
- **Boiling point:** 167–168 °C (332–334 °F).

### 2. Limonene (d-limonene)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Bright, sharp citrus — lemon and orange peel; sometimes faintly bitter.
- **Effects:** Mood-elevating, anxiolytic, anti-stress, antifungal. Russo (2011) cites Komori et al. (1995): hospitalized depressed patients exposed to citrus fragrance normalized Hamilton Depression Scores and 9/12 discontinued antidepressants.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Most common in sativas and citrus-forward hybrids.
  - **Sativa:** Super Lemon Haze, Tangie, Lemon Haze, Jack Herer (secondary), Durban Poison (secondary).
  - **Hybrid:** Wedding Cake, Do-Si-Dos, MAC, Berry White, White Fire OG.
  - **Indica:** Banana OG, Purple Hindu Kush (minor).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Lemon, lime, orange, grapefruit peel; juniper, peppermint, rosemary.
- **Boiling point:** 176–177 °C (348–351 °F).

### 3. β-Caryophyllene (BCP)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Spicy, peppery, woody, clove-like, with diesel/fuel undertones in cannabis.
- **Effects:** **The only common terpene that selectively binds CB2 receptors** (Gertsch et al. 2008, *PNAS*; Ki ≈ 155 nM), making it functionally a dietary cannabinoid with documented anti-inflammatory, analgesic, anxiolytic, and gastroprotective activity. Non-psychoactive (no CB1 binding). FDA GRAS as food flavoring.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Pervasive — found in nearly every chemovar; especially heavy in Cookies, Chem/Diesel, OG, and Kush families.
  - **Indica:** Bubba Kush, Purple Punch, GMO/Garlic Cookies, Skywalker OG.
  - **Hybrid:** GSC (Girl Scout Cookies), Gelato, Wedding Cake, OG Kush.
  - **Sativa:** Sour Diesel, Chemdog, Original Glue (GG4).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Black pepper, cloves, cinnamon, rosemary, hops, oregano, basil, copaiba.
- **Boiling point:** ~266 °C (480 °F) at atmospheric pressure (one of the highest of any cannabis terpene; survives high-temp processing).

### 4. Linalool
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Floral lavender, slightly spicy and citrus-sweet; sometimes blueberry-tinged.
- **Effects:** Sedative, anxiolytic, anticonvulsant, analgesic. Russo (2011), citing Nunes et al. (2010): "the modulation of glutamate and GABA neurotransmitter systems are likely … responsible for the sedative, anxiolytic and anticonvulsant properties of linalool."
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Indica-leaning floral cultivars and dessert hybrids.
  - **Indica:** Granddaddy Purple, Bubba Kush, Lavender Kush, Do-Si-Dos, Master Kush, Kosher Kush.
  - **Hybrid:** LA Confidential, Amnesia Haze, Wedding Cake, Zkittlez.
  - **Sativa:** Pink Kush (some phenotypes), Special Kush.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Lavender, rosewood, basil, mint, cinnamon, coriander, rose, birch.
- **Boiling point:** 198 °C (388 °F).

### 5. α-Pinene and β-Pinene
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Sharp, fresh pine, fir needles, rosemary; α-pinene reads crisp and immediate, β-pinene reads more resinous/damp-wood.
- **Effects:** Bronchodilator (Falk et al. 1990, cited in Russo 2011), anti-inflammatory, alertness/memory support via acetylcholinesterase inhibition (Miyazawa & Yamafuji 2005, IC50 0.44 mM) — proposed by Russo as a partial antidote to THC-induced short-term memory deficit. Antimicrobial: pinene-rich *Sideritis* essential oils inhibited MRSA comparably to vancomycin (Kose et al. 2010).
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Frequently in sativas with daytime, focus-oriented profiles.
  - **Sativa:** Jack Herer, Strawberry Cough, Snowcap, Dutch Treat, Big Smooth.
  - **Hybrid:** Blue Dream, Trainwreck, Cannatonic (CBD strain), OG Kush (secondary).
  - **Indica:** Critical Mass, Romulan (secondary).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Pine needles, rosemary, sage, eucalyptus, conifer resin, orange peel.
- **Boiling point:** α-pinene 155–156 °C (311–313 °F); β-pinene 165–166 °C (329–331 °F).

### 6. Terpinolene
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Complex — floral, piney, herbal, citrusy, slightly woody; "lilac with apple."
- **Effects:** Paradoxical. Isolated terpinolene shows sedative and antioxidant activity in rodents (Ito & Ito 2013) and prevents LDL oxidation, but in living cannabis chemovars it correlates strongly with **uplifting, cerebral, creative** effects via entourage interactions. Dominant in only ~10% of cultivars (Leafly database aggregate) — a connoisseur signature.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Almost exclusively sativa-dominant or sativa-leaning hybrids.
  - **Sativa:** Jack Herer (~0.5%), Durban Poison (often >0.6%), Ghost Train Haze, Golden Goat, Dutch Treat, Super Lemon Haze, XJ-13, Chocolope.
  - **Hybrid:** Clementine, Pineapple Express.
  - **Indica:** Afghani (notable exception; terpinolene-rich landrace indica).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Nutmeg, tea tree, lilac, cumin, apples, conifers.
- **Boiling point:** 184–186 °C (~366 °F).

### 7. Humulene (α-humulene, formerly α-caryophyllene)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Earthy, woody, hoppy — the smell of a quality IPA — with a peppery edge.
- **Effects:** Anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antibacterial, and uniquely an **appetite suppressant** — unusual among cannabis compounds (most stimulate appetite). Co-occurs biosynthetically with β-caryophyllene.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Across the spectrum; heavy in Kush genetics and Cookies family.
  - **Indica:** Bubba Kush, Hindu Kush, Skywalker OG, Pink Kush.
  - **Hybrid:** GSC, White Widow, Headband, Death Star, Original Glue.
  - **Sativa:** Sour Diesel, Super Lemon Haze, Candyland.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Hops, sage, ginseng, black pepper, ginger, clove, basil.
- **Boiling point:** ~198 °C (388 °F) at atmospheric pressure; The Leaf Online lists C₁₅H₂₄ humulene at 198 °C / 388 °F; some sources cite 106–107 °C at reduced pressure.

### 8. Ocimene (α- and β-)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Sweet, herbaceous, woody with mint and faint citrus; the dominant note in basil.
- **Effects:** Uplifting, energizing, decongestant; antiviral and antifungal in vitro. Acyclic monoterpene; one of the **most volatile** cannabis terpenes — among the first lost to heat.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Stronger in sativas and hybrids.
  - **Sativa:** Strawberry Cough, Sour Diesel, Chocolope, Purple Haze, Amnesia.
  - **Hybrid:** Dutch Treat, Golden Goat, Space Queen, Clementine.
  - **Indica:** Himalayan Gold (less common).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Basil, mint, parsley, mango, kumquat, bergamot, tarragon, orchids, hops.
- **Boiling point:** 65–66 °C (~150 °F) at reduced pressure; atmospheric ~100 °C (~212 °F).

### 9. Borneol
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Camphor, menthol, herbal-woody with a faint metallic note; "moxa."
- **Effects:** Analgesic, antibiotic, anti-inflammatory; used in traditional Chinese medicine since ~1600 ("moxa") for inflammation, anxiety, heart disease. Positively modulates GABA-A receptors (Granger et al. 2005). Acts as a penetration enhancer at the blood-brain barrier.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Secondary terpene, ~0.01–0.05% of profile, slightly biased to high-THC strains.
  - **Hybrid:** OG Kush, Golden Haze, K-13 Haze.
  - **Sativa:** Amnesia Haze.
  - **Indica:** Hindu Kush (trace).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Camphor, rosemary, mint, thyme, ginger, sage, *Dryobalanops aromatica*.
- **Boiling point:** 210–213 °C (410–415 °F).

### 10. α-Bisabolol (levomenol)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Soft, sweet floral with chamomile, honey, and faint nutty undertones.
- **Effects:** Anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, analgesic, skin-soothing, wound-healing. Activates T-type calcium channels (Gadotti et al. 2021) — a shared mechanism with THC at Cav3.2 for inflammatory/neuropathic pain.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Found in indica and hybrid strains marketed for relaxation; secondary terpene.
  - **Indica:** Pink Kush, Master Kush, Granddaddy Purple.
  - **Hybrid:** Headband, Harle-Tsu (CBD strain), ACDC.
  - **Sativa:** Oracle, Cherry Pie phenotypes (variable).
- **Food/Plant sources:** German chamomile, candeia tree, sage, some lavenders.
- **Boiling point:** ~153 °C at 12 mmHg; atmospheric ~314 °C (597 °F). High thermal stability.

### 11. Nerolidol (trans-nerolidol / cis-nerolidol)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Floral-woody with citrus, apple, and faint jasmine/lemongrass.
- **Effects:** Sedative, anxiolytic, antimicrobial, antimalarial, antifungal, antiparasitic; powerful skin-penetration enhancer (useful for transdermal cannabinoid delivery). **Trans-nerolidol was the most statistically significant terpene correlate of anxiety relief** in Kamal et al. (2018, *Front Neurosci*): "Tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and trans-nerolidol have statistically significant correlations with increased anxiolytic activity."
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Secondary terpene; strong in Kush-family genetics.
  - **Indica:** Bubba Kush, Skywalker OG, Kosher Kush, Master Kush.
  - **Hybrid:** Island Sweet Skunk, GSC, Sweet Skunk.
  - **Sativa:** Island Jack Herer, Super Skunk.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Jasmine, lavender, tea tree, ginger, lemongrass, neroli (orange blossom), oolong tea, *Brassavola nodosa*.
- **Boiling point:** ~122 °C at 1 mmHg; atmospheric ~276 °C (~529 °F).

### 12. Geraniol
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Sweet rose, geranium, with peachy/citrus notes.
- **Effects:** Neuroprotective, antioxidant, antimicrobial, antiviral, antitumor (preclinical), mosquito repellent. Dose-dependent in rodent models (~200 mg/kg).
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Minor terpene across categories.
  - **Sativa:** Amnesia Haze, Great White Shark, Headband.
  - **Hybrid:** Lavender, Island Sweet Skunk, OG Shark.
  - **Indica:** Afghani, Master Kush.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Rose oil, geraniums, lemongrass, citronella, tobacco, peaches, plums.
- **Boiling point:** 229–230 °C (~445 °F).

### 13. Valencene
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Sweet, juicy citrus — Valencia orange, tangerine, grapefruit. Rounder than limonene.
- **Effects:** Anti-inflammatory, potential anti-allergic; documented insect repellent (mosquitoes, ticks). Mood-elevating in entourage context. Precursor to nootkatone (grapefruit aroma) via oxidation.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Uncommon as a primary terpene; appears in citrus-genetic sativas/hybrids.
  - **Sativa/Hybrid:** Tangie, Clementine, Agent Orange, Lime Skittles, Sour Diesel (trace).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Valencia oranges, tangerines, grapefruit, mango.
- **Boiling point:** ~123 °C at reduced pressure; atmospheric ~254 °C (489 °F).

### 14. Camphene
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Pungent, musky, damp earth and fir needles; often confused with myrcene.
- **Effects:** Anti-inflammatory, antioxidant; **lipid-lowering** — Vallianou et al. (2011, *PLOS ONE*) showed significant reductions in total cholesterol, LDL, and triglycerides in hyperlipidemic rats. Inhibits Cav3.2 T-type calcium channels with α-bisabolol for neuropathic pain (Gadotti et al. 2021).
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** More common in indica-leaning, Kush-derived genetics.
  - **Indica:** Mendocino Purps, Hindu Kush, Ghost OG, Strawberry Banana.
  - **Hybrid:** Skywalker OG, ACDC, Banana Kush.
  - **Sativa:** Less common.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Camphor, cypress, fir, valerian, neroli, ginger, rosemary, citronella.
- **Boiling point:** 158–160 °C (~318 °F); solid/crystalline at room temperature.

### 15. Sabinene
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Spicy, peppery, piney with citrus and faint mint — the heat of black pepper.
- **Effects:** Anti-inflammatory, antifungal, antimicrobial, antioxidant; may aid digestion and saliva production.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Minor terpene; tends to appear in spicy/citrusy chemovars.
  - **Sativa:** Super Lemon Haze, Super Silver Haze.
  - **Hybrid:** Orange Bud, Diesel-family crosses.
  - **Indica:** Northern Lights (low concentrations).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Holm oak, Norway spruce, black pepper, nutmeg, carrot seed, clove, cardamom, tea tree.
- **Boiling point:** ~163–164 °C (325 °F).

### 16. Pulegone
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Strong peppermint, spearmint, camphor.
- **Effects:** Memory protection — preclinical work suggests pulegone inhibits acetylcholinesterase, potentially counteracting THC-related short-term memory deficits (similar mechanism to α-pinene). Mucolytic, mild sedative. **Safety note:** at high concentrations pulegone is hepatotoxic (linked to FDA warnings on pennyroyal oil); cannabis-level traces are far below toxic thresholds.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Trace-level in mint-scented chemovars across categories.
  - **Hybrid:** Headband, Chocolope (trace), some Mints crosses.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Pennyroyal, peppermint, spearmint, rosemary, blackcurrant.
- **Boiling point:** ~224 °C (435 °F).

### 17. Guaiol
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Pine, wood, rose; a sesquiterpenoid alcohol (liquid, not oily).
- **Effects:** Anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, insecticidal; anti-parasitic against *Leishmania* in vitro; supports diuresis. **Note:** Kamal et al. (2018) found guaiol *negatively* correlated with anxiety relief — possibly anxiogenic in some users.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Secondary terpene; pine-heavy strains.
  - **Hybrid:** Liberty Haze, Royal Gorilla (GG#4), Chocolope, Sour Diesel.
  - **Sativa:** Haze Berry.
  - **Indica:** Kali Dog, Sour Berry, BC Sensi Star.
- **Food/Plant sources:** *Guaiacum* tree, cypress pine, nutmeg, apples, cumin, ginger, ginseng, valerian, tea tree.
- **Boiling point:** ~92 °C (~198 °F) — one of the lowest in cannabis.

### 18. Eucalyptol (1,8-cineole)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Cool, minty, eucalyptus, camphor.
- **Effects:** Bronchodilator, mucolytic, nasal decongestant; analgesic and anti-inflammatory; acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (memory/Alzheimer's research interest); studied for COPD and asthma. Antibacterial. Only ~0.06% of cannabis strains express it meaningfully.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Rare across categories.
  - **Sativa:** Super Silver Haze.
  - **Hybrid:** Headband, GSC, ACDC.
  - **Indica:** Bubba Kush (some phenotypes).
- **Food/Plant sources:** Eucalyptus (~80% of essential oil), tea tree, rosemary, sage, sweet basil, bay leaves, wormwood, mugwort.
- **Boiling point:** 176 °C (349 °F).

### 19. α-Terpineol
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Soft lilac, apple blossom, clove, pine-smoke (lapsang souchong tea note).
- **Effects:** Sedative, anxiolytic, antibiotic, antioxidant, antimalarial; acetylcholinesterase inhibitor. Often co-occurs with α-pinene but is masked by it.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Cross-category; often in pine-forward strains.
  - **Indica:** OG Kush, GSC, Hindu Kush.
  - **Hybrid:** Jack Herer, White Widow.
  - **Sativa:** Lemon Skunk, Super Lemon Haze.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Pine, lilac, eucalyptus sap, bitter orange tree, cajuput oil, lapsang tea.
- **Boiling point:** 217–219 °C (422–426 °F).

### 20. trans-Nerolidol *(see #11 for full profile)*
Listed separately by the user's brief; "trans-nerolidol" is the dominant geometric isomer in cannabis and is the form responsible for Kamal et al.'s (2018) anxiolytic chemotype finding. The cis form is rarer and slightly more sedating; the trans form leans calming-but-clear. Same plant sources, same approximate boiling point (~276 °C / 529 °F at atmospheric pressure); same Kush-family strain associations (Bubba Kush, Skywalker OG, Kosher Kush).

### 21. Phytol
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Cut grass, green tea, balsam; faintly floral.
- **Effects:** Anxiolytic and sedative (mouse models, GABAergic mechanism), anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antioxidant. Precursor in vitamin E and K synthesis; a degradation product of chlorophyll. **Safety caveat:** Schwotzer et al. (2021, *Inhalation Toxicology* 33:33–40) reported in "Phytol, not propylene glycol, causes severe pulmonary injury after inhalation dosing in Sprague-Dawley rats" — phytol should **not** be used as a vape additive, though naturally occurring trace phytol in flower is not implicated.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** A degradation product of chlorophyll, so present at low levels across most well-cured cannabis.
  - **Sativa:** Sour Diesel, Jack Herer.
  - **Hybrid:** Cheese, OG Shark.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Green tea, matcha, raspberries, parsnips, white cabbage, tobacco, eggplant.
- **Boiling point:** ~203–204 °C (~399 °F).

### 22. Phellandrene (α- and β-)
- **Aroma/Flavor:** Peppery-mint with citrus and pine; sharp, herbal.
- **Effects:** Analgesic (neuropathic and inflammatory pain models), anti-inflammatory, antifungal (effective against *Penicillium cyclopium*), immunostimulant in murine studies. Used in Eastern medicine for centuries. Note: undiluted α-phellandrene can cause mild skin irritation.
- **Indica/Sativa/Hybrid:** Secondary, often appearing alongside terpinolene.
  - **Sativa:** Jack Herer, Trainwreck, Durban Poison, Super Lemon Haze, Ace of Spades.
  - **Hybrid:** Lemon Skunk, SAGE.
- **Food/Plant sources:** Eucalyptus, dill, black pepper, mint, parsley, cinnamon, lavender, pine, ginger grass, water fennel.
- **Boiling point:** α-phellandrene ~171 °C (~340 °F); β-phellandrene ~175 °C (~347 °F).

## Terpene Synergies — The Entourage Effect

The "entourage effect" — the hypothesis that cannabis cannabinoids and terpenes interact to produce effects greater than (or different from) either alone — was formalized by Ethan Russo (2011, *Br J Pharmacol* 163:1344–1364, "Taming THC: potential cannabis synergy and phytocannabinoid-terpenoid entourage effects"). Russo's individual combination claims are framed as hypotheses ("could contribute," "may produce") rather than proven RCT findings; subsequent observational research (Kamal et al. 2018) and the 2019 Russo follow-up (*Front Plant Sci* 9:1969) have begun to validate specific combinations.

**Important counterweight:** Finlay, Sircombe, Nimick, Jones & Glass (2020, *Frontiers in Pharmacology*, "Terpenoids From Cannabis Do Not Mediate an Entourage Effect by Acting at Cannabinoid Receptors") concluded "no data were produced to support the hypothesis that any of the five terpenes tested (either alone or in mixtures) have direct interactions with CB1 or CB2." This suggests entourage effects operate through complementary downstream pathways (5-HT1A, GABA-A, TRP, AChE, immune modulation), not additive receptor binding. Treat the pairings below as evidence-informed, not prescriptive.

### Myrcene + Limonene
- **Effect:** Balanced relaxation with mood lift — sedation tempered by citrus uplift. The classic "evening unwind without sleep yet" profile.
- **Strain template:** Blue Dream, Wedding Cake, Do-Si-Dos.

### Myrcene + Linalool (+ optional β-caryophyllene)
- **Effect:** **The sleep stack.** Myrcene's muscle-relaxant and sedative action stacks with linalool's GABAergic anxiolysis; adding β-caryophyllene quiets CB2-mediated inflammation that disrupts sleep.
- **Strain template:** Granddaddy Purple, Northern Lights, Lavender Kush, Bubba Kush.
- **Russo (2011) verbatim:** "Myrcene… combined with THC may produce the 'couch-lock' phenomenon of certain chemotypes." And: "the modulation of glutamate and GABA neurotransmitter systems are likely to be the critical mechanism responsible for the sedative, anxiolytic and anticonvulsant properties of linalool."

### Linalool + β-Caryophyllene
- **Effect:** Calm-bodied pain relief without heavy sedation; the foundation of "relaxing but functional" hybrid effects. Linalool quiets the mind via GABA; caryophyllene quiets the body via CB2.
- **Strain template:** GSC, Wedding Cake, Bubba Kush.
- **Russo (2019) verbatim:** Notes a "Caryodiol™" chemovar (high caryophyllene + high CBD, ratio 1:39.4 THC:CBD with 0.83% caryophyllene) as proof-of-concept "applicable to treatment of numerous clinical conditions including: pain, inflammation, fibrotic disorders, addiction, anxiety, depression, autoimmune diseases, dermatological conditions and cancer."

### α-Pinene + Myrcene
- **Effect:** Counters cannabis "fog." α-Pinene's acetylcholinesterase inhibition (Miyazawa & Yamafuji 2005, IC50 0.44 mM) preserves short-term memory while myrcene anchors a relaxed body state — a "thinking person's indica" profile.
- **Strain template:** Blue Dream, OG Kush, Trainwreck.
- **Russo (2011) verbatim:** "[α-pinene's AChE inhibition] could counteract short-term memory deficits induced by THC intoxication."

### α-Pinene + Limonene (+ Terpinolene)
- **Effect:** **Daytime focus and creativity stack.** Pinene sharpens, limonene elevates mood, terpinolene contributes cerebral creativity. Classic uplifting sativa effect.
- **Strain template:** Jack Herer, Durban Poison, Super Lemon Haze.

### β-Caryophyllene + Humulene
- **Effect:** Sesquiterpene-driven anti-inflammatory + analgesic relief, with appetite modulation. Because humulene is one of the few terpenes that *suppresses* appetite, this pairing can blunt THC-induced munchies while preserving pain relief.
- **Strain template:** Sour Diesel, White Widow, Headband.

### β-Caryophyllene + Limonene (+ Humulene) — The "Balance Blend"
- **Effect:** Anti-inflammatory + mood elevation + light appetite control; functional hybrid profile.
- **Strain template:** GSC, Gelato, OG Kush, Skywalker OG.

### trans-Nerolidol + β-Caryophyllene + Limonene — The "Kamal Anxiolytic Chemotype"
- **Effect:** The strongest evidence-backed combination for anxiety relief in real-world cannabis. Kamal et al. (2018, *Front Neurosci* 12:730) verbatim: "Three of the four most effective strains were 'Kush' varieties… with high levels of trans-nerolidol, β-Caryophyllene, and D-limonene… In the most effective strains, trans-nerolidol was the most abundant, followed by myrcene and β-Caryophyllene." Trans-nerolidol was the only terpene with statistically significant anxiolytic correlation.
- **Strain template:** Bubba Kush, Skywalker OG, Kosher Kush.

### β-Caryophyllene + CBD
- **Effect:** Non-psychoactive pain, inflammation, and addiction-cessation support. Russo (2011) verbatim: "CBD, with caryophyllene, and possibly other adjunctive terpenoids in the extract, offers significant promise in future addiction treatment." Both compounds engage CB2 / immune pathways; CBD also engages 5-HT1A.
- **Strain template:** ACDC, Harlequin, Cannatonic.

### α-Pinene + CBG/CBD (The MRSA Stack)
- **Effect:** Antimicrobial synergy. Russo (2011) cites Appendino et al. (2008): "Pure CBD and CBG powerfully inhibit MRSA (MIC 0.5–2 µg·mL⁻¹)," and Kose et al. (2010): pinene-rich essential oils matched vancomycin against MRSA. A CBG-chemotype expressing pinene "would seem to offer an excellent, safe new antiseptic agent."
- **Strain template:** CBG-dominant cultivars like White CBG, Jack Frost CBG.

### Linalool + Limonene + CBD
- **Effect:** Anti-anxiety and antidepressant action. Russo (2011) verbatim: "Addition of anxiolytic limonene and linalool could contribute to the clinical efficacy of a CBD extract." Komori et al. (1995) demonstrated citrus-fragrance-only normalized Hamilton Depression Scores in hospitalized patients.
- **Strain template:** High-CBD hybrids like Harle-Tsu, Ringo's Gift.

### Eucalyptol + α-Pinene
- **Effect:** Respiratory support — bronchodilation and mucolytic action useful for asthma/sinus congestion; both compounds also serve as cholinesterase inhibitors (cognitive/Alzheimer's interest).
- **Strain template:** Super Silver Haze, Headband.

### Terpinolene + Limonene + Ocimene
- **Effect:** The "uplift trio" — sharp citrus + herbal-floral + sweet-volatile head rush. Best at low vape temperatures (ocimene escapes early). Daytime creativity profile.
- **Strain template:** Jack Herer, Durban Poison, Strawberry Cough, Golden Goat.

### Bisabolol + Linalool + Caryophyllene — The "Skin/Topical Stack"
- **Effect:** Anti-inflammatory, anti-irritant, calming; ideal in topicals. Bisabolol enhances skin penetration; linalool soothes; caryophyllene addresses underlying CB2 inflammation.
- **Strain template:** Pink Kush, Master Kush (selected for topical extraction).

### Nerolidol + Linalool (Topical Penetration Enhancers)
- **Effect:** Both are skin-penetration enhancers, useful as carriers for transdermal cannabinoid delivery; both add sedative-floral character. Common in nighttime topicals and sleep balms.

### CBD + Linalool + Caryophyllene (Russo 2019 Anticonvulsant Stack)
- **Effect:** Russo (2019) verbatim: "Such observations support the hypothesis of greater efficacy for Cannabis extracts combining multiple anticonvulsant components, such as CBD, THC, THCA, THCV, CBDV, linalool, and even caryophyllene (Lewis et al., 2018)." Relevant for refractory epilepsy adjunct use.

## Recommendations

### For Budtenders / Retail
1. **Lead with the dominant terpene, not the indica/sativa label.** Train staff to read the top two or three terpenes on the COA and match them to customer goals: myrcene/linalool → sleep; limonene/pinene → daytime focus; nerolidol/caryophyllene/limonene → anxiety; caryophyllene/myrcene → pain.
2. **Shop by total terpene content too.** Below ~1% total terpenes, effect modulation is subtle; above ~2%, terpene character dominates the experience.
3. **Build a deli-jar "smell test" workflow.** Pepper-clove = caryophyllene; mango-musk = myrcene; pine-rosemary = pinene; lavender-floral = linalool; lemon-zest = limonene; cut-grass = phytol/ocimene; chamomile-honey = bisabolol; camphor-menthol = borneol or eucalyptol.

### For Personal Grow & Strain Selection
1. **Cool nighttime temps + UV-B + moderate late-flower drought stress** increase terpene production — but harvest before terpene peaks decline (camphene and ocimene degrade fastest).
2. **Cure slow, store cold.** Nitrogen-flushed jars below 15 °C (60 °F) preserve monoterpenes (myrcene, limonene, ocimene). Sesquiterpenes (caryophyllene, humulene, bisabolol) are far more stable.
3. **Targets by goal:**
   - Anxiety/sleep: Kush-family genetics (Bubba Kush, Skywalker OG, Kosher Kush) for the trans-nerolidol + caryophyllene + limonene anxiolytic chemotype.
   - Daytime focus: terpinolene + pinene dominants (Jack Herer, Durban Poison).
   - Pain/inflammation: caryophyllene-dominant cultivars (Garlic Cookies, OG Kush, Original Glue).
   - Memory protection: pinene-forward strains (Jack Herer, Big Smooth) alongside moderate THC.

### Vaporizer Temperature Strategy
- **Low (155–175 °C / 311–347 °F):** Captures pinene, ocimene, myrcene, limonene, eucalyptol — bright, clear, flavor-forward.
- **Medium (175–200 °C / 347–392 °F):** Adds linalool, humulene, terpineol — fuller body, sedation builds.
- **High (200–230 °C / 392–446 °F):** Adds geraniol, pulegone, phytol — heavier, "couch-locking" zone.
- **Very high (>230 °C / 446 °F):** Captures the heaviest sesquiterpenes (caryophyllene, bisabolol) and approaches cannabinoid combustion; flavor degrades but full pharmacology is delivered.

### Benchmarks That Should Change Your Recommendation
- If a customer reports anxiety on cannabis: avoid high-terpinolene + high-THC ("racy" combo) and high-guaiol; steer toward trans-nerolidol/caryophyllene/limonene Kush chemovars.
- If "couch-lock" is unwanted: avoid myrcene >0.5%; favor pinene/terpinolene-led sativas.
- If THC tolerance is high but cognition matters: prioritize α-pinene ≥0.3% alongside moderate THC for AChE-mediated memory protection.
- If appetite suppression is the goal: prioritize humulene-forward strains (Sour Diesel, White Widow); avoid myrcene-dominant indicas.

## Caveats

1. **Most terpene effect claims are preclinical.** With the partial exception of β-caryophyllene (CB2 binding, Gertsch et al. 2008, *PNAS*) and aromatherapy-grade linalool/limonene anxiety studies, the medicinal effects described are extrapolated from animal models, in vitro assays, or essential-oil studies — not from clinical trials of cannabis.
2. **Russo's pairings are hypotheses, not prescriptions.** The 2011 *Br J Pharmacol* paper uses conditional language ("could contribute," "may produce"). The 2019 follow-up reaffirms the framework with the Caryodiol™ chemovar as concrete proof-of-concept but explicitly notes "the case for Cannabis synergy via the 'entourage effect' is currently sufficiently strong as to suggest that one molecule is unlikely to match the therapeutic and even industrial potential of Cannabis itself" — i.e., still building, not yet definitive.
3. **Direct receptor synergy is contested.** Finlay et al. (2020, *Frontiers in Pharmacology*) reported "no data were produced to support the hypothesis that any of the five terpenes tested (either alone or in mixtures) have direct interactions with CB1 or CB2." Entourage effects, if real, likely operate via complementary downstream pathways, not additive receptor binding.
4. **Strain-name lookup is unreliable.** Smith, Vergara, Keegan & Jikomes (2022, *PLOS ONE* 17:e0267498, ~90,000 samples) found "commercial labels do not consistently align with the observed chemical diversity." Identical strain names from different growers can have radically different terpene profiles. Always defer to the **batch-specific COA**.
5. **Boiling points reported across sources vary by ±5–10 °C** depending on measurement pressure (atmospheric vs. reduced pressure). Treat numbers as practical vape guidance, not laboratory absolutes.
6. **Indica/sativa labels are inherited from morphology and convention, not chemistry.** Use COAs.
7. **Pulegone and phytol carry specific safety notes.** Pulegone is hepatotoxic at high concentrations (pennyroyal-oil dosing, far beyond cannabis levels). Phytol should **not** be used as a vape additive — Schwotzer et al. (2021, *Inhalation Toxicology* 33:33–40) demonstrated severe pulmonary injury from phytol inhalation in rats.
8. **Kamal et al. (2018) is observational with a small n.** The trans-nerolidol anxiety finding (p < 0.05) and the β-caryophyllene trend (p = 0.08) are correlational, based on the four most/least effective strains in a patient-rated dataset — not a randomized trial. Treat as the best available evidence for anxiety-strain selection, but not as RCT proof.
